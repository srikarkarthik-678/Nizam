Introduction

Purani Haveli — The Old Palace

✦

In the heart of Hyderabad's Old City stands Purani Haveli — meaning "Old Mansion" — a palace whose origins trace to the Qutub Shahi era. Built in the 18th-century European architectural style, it features ornate facades, the Ayina Khaana (mirror hall), and Chinese glass rooms, shaped like the letter "U" with two long wings flanking the central residence.

The Nizam's Museum, established in February 2000, displays the extraordinary gifts presented to Mir Osman Ali Khan, Asaf Jah VII — the last Nizam — during his Silver Jubilee in 1936. At its height this dynasty presided over one of the wealthiest kingdoms on earth, the Nizam's fortune estimated at $230 billion in today's value.

"Walking into Purani Haveli is not entering a museum. It is entering a memory — preserved in wood, silk, silver, and stone."

The Rulers

From Qutub Shahi to Asaf Jahi

✦

Two great dynasties shaped the Deccan. The Qutub Shahi Dynasty (1518–1687), founded by Sultan Quli Qutub-ul-Mulk, ruled from Golconda Fort and founded the city of Hyderabad in 1591. Seven rulers governed under this dynasty, patronising art, architecture, and the world-famed Golconda diamond trade.

✦

The Asaf Jahi Dynasty (1724–1948) — the Nizams — was founded by Mir Qamaruddin Khan Nizam-ul-Mulk. Seven Nizams ruled over one of the world's most prosperous kingdoms. The Sixth Nizam, Mir Mahbub Ali Khan, was legendary for his taste in fashion. The Seventh, Mir Osman Ali Khan, was among the wealthiest individuals in all of human history.

✦

The five artefacts presented here are windows into this extraordinary legacy — each object a chapter of a royal life lived with intelligence, artistry, and unrivalled grandeur.

The Nizam's Walk-in Wardrobe

World's Longest Royal Dressing Hall  ·  176 Feet of Burma Teak

Imagine being a royal servant walking through this endless corridor — not a hallway, but a wardrobe filled with the king's clothes. This is a space not of containment, but of ceremony.

MaterialBurma Teak — prized for moisture resistance and structural durability
Length176 feet — one of the world's longest walk-in wardrobes
LevelsTwo-tiered with a hand-cranked wooden elevator — among India's oldest surviving mechanical lifts
Built ForNawab Mir Mahbub Ali Khan, the Sixth Nizam of Hyderabad

From an engineering perspective, this wardrobe demonstrates remarkable intelligence in spatial design. The long, symmetrical two-level layout allows efficient access while wooden cabinets regulate temperature and moisture — an early form of climate-controlled textile preservation. Closed panels block dust and UV light, preventing colour fading in delicate silks.

The sixth Nizam reportedly never wore the same outfit twice. Garments were gifted away after a single use. The wardrobe was thus not merely storage but a living archive of royal fashion — and a feat of 19th-century material science.

"This is not just a wardrobe — it is a blend of architecture, science, and royal lifestyle, showing how even storage was designed with intelligence and elegance."

The Royal Garments

Achkan  ·  Sherwani  ·  Sarees — Preserved Stories of Royal Life

Imagine opening one of these wooden cabinets and finding the personal clothes of the Nizam — each outfit carefully preserved, reflecting royalty and elegance across centuries.

AchkanZar-e-baft (gold-woven) fabric · 19th Century · Hyderabad
SherwaniKim Khab brocade with metallic zari · 19th Century · Hyderabad
SareeFine Chikankari shadow-embroidery on sheer muslin
PreservationTeak cabinet humidity control · dust exclusion · restricted light

Each garment is a marvel of material science and craft engineering. Zar-e-baft fabric uses real gold and silver threads interwoven with silk — simultaneously flexible and radiant. Kim Khab brocades are woven on handlooms using heavy silk with metallic zari threads, requiring thousands of weft passes per inch.

The Chikankari saree displays extraordinarily fine shadow embroidery on sheer muslin, surviving over 150 years owing to the wardrobe's engineered environment. Royal fashion was identity — each garment announced status, origin, and occasion simultaneously.

From a scientific perspective, each fabric is a feat of material engineering. Zar-e-baft achieves the paradox of being simultaneously rigid in appearance yet fluid in movement, owing to the precise ratio of metallic to silk threads. Kim Khab's weight and density gave it natural insulating properties suited to the Deccan climate, while the translucency of the Chikankari saree's muslin base was achieved by spinning cotton to an almost impossibly fine count — a textile so delicate it was once described as "woven air."

What preserved these garments across more than a century and a half is the wardrobe itself. The Burma Teak enclosures regulate humidity, preventing the expansion and contraction of fibres that causes thread breakage over time. Restricted light slows the photochemical degradation of natural dyes, and the spacing between garments ensures air circulation — a passive, architecture-driven conservation system that modern museum storage still replicates in principle."

Royal fashion in the Nizam's court was never merely aesthetic — it was a language. The choice of fabric, colour, and cut communicated rank, occasion, and allegiance. An Achkan in gold-woven brocade worn to a formal durbar carried a different message from a linen sherwa worn at court for everyday affairs. Today, these garments survive not only as objects of beauty but as a record of a court culture where every thread was deliberate, every weave intentional, and every garment a statement of who the Nizam was — and intended to remain."

Each garment displayed within the Nizam's wardrobe is far more than clothing — it is a document of power, taste, and civilisation. The collection includes the Achkan, a formal coat woven in Zar-e-baft — a fabric threaded with real gold and silver — the Sherwani in Kim Khab brocade, a heavy silk interlaced with metallic zari requiring thousands of weft passes per inch, and a Chikankari saree bearing the finest shadow embroidery on sheer muslin, a craft developed over centuries in the ateliers of Lucknow and Hyderabad.

"These are not just clothes — they are preserved stories of royal life, protected through smart design and timeless craftsmanship."

The Ittardan — Royal Perfume Ensemble

A Functional Sculpture  ·  Fine Silver Filigree  ·  The Tree of Abundance

After wearing his royal achkan, the Nizam's dressing ritual was not complete without this — the final, invisible touch. A fragrance was not applied. It was gifted to the air itself.

MaterialFine silver filigree with gold-plated accents and hand-painted glass cups
ProvenanceGifted by the Raja of Palvancha · Silver Jubilee, 1936
SymbolismTree of prosperity · coin-shaped leaves · Krishna with flute at centre
Ittar ScienceSteam distillation (deg-bhapka) in copper vessels · sandalwood oil base

This is not simply a perfume stand — it is a miniature sculptural installation. A tree-like silver structure symbolising prosperity supports multiple glass fragrance cups. The central figure, Krishna with a flute, adds divine and cultural resonance. The base resembles a fort courtyard — the Nizam's own kingdom rendered in miniature.

The science of ittar is remarkable. Traditional attars are produced through deg-bhapka distillation — a copper-vessel technique where botanical materials (roses, jasmine, oud) are steam-distilled into sandalwood oil. Glass containers prevent oxidation, preserving the scent for centuries.

"Royal clothing gave the Nizam his appearance — but fragrance gave him presence. Together, they created a complete symbol of power and elegance."

The Coins of the Deccan

Bahmani  ·  Adil Shahi  ·  Qutub Shahi  ·  Osmania — From Bidar to Hyderabad

Imagine the same Nizam who wore royal clothes and royal perfumes — using these very coins to govern a powerful economy. Every coin is a small, circular history book.

Bahmani CoinsCopper · 16th Century · Bidar
Adil Shahi CoinsCopper · 16th Century · Bijapur
Qutub Shahi CoinCopper · 16th Century · Golconda
Abdullah Qutub ShahCopper · 17th Century · Hyderabad
Nizam VI CoinSilver · 1901 · Hyderabad
Osmania CoinsGerman Silver, Silver & Copper · 20th Century

From a scientific perspective, coin minting in the Deccan was sophisticated metallurgy. Copper and silver were smelted, alloyed for hardness, and struck with engraved steel dies. Persian and Arabic inscriptions were cut using burin tools — an early and effective form of anti-counterfeiting technology.

The Osmania currency carried its own exchange rate across the Deccan. "German Silver" Osmania coins were a deliberate silver-copper-nickel alloy balancing durability, appearance, and cost. These coins span 600 years of Deccan commerce, each funding the palaces, wardrobes, perfumes, and arts that defined royal Hyderabad.

What makes this collection particularly significant is the unbroken narrative of succession it represents. From the rough copper coins of the Bahmani Sultanate to the finely struck silver Osmania pieces of the 20th century, each dynasty built upon the monetary and cultural legacy of the last. The inscription on a Qutub Shahi coin from Golconda and the inscription on an Osmania coin from Hyderabad are separated by over three centuries — yet both assert, in different scripts and different metals, the same truth: that sovereignty is written in currency, and that the Deccan was always a civilisation that knew its own worth.

"These coins are not just money — they are symbols of power, economy, and the very foundation of the Nizam's royal lifestyle."

The Living Portrait

Reverse Stained Glass  ·  Diamonds, Sapphires & Emeralds  ·  The Eyes That Follow You

As you stand before this portrait, something extraordinary occurs — the Nizam's gaze finds you wherever you stand. His eyes follow. His shoes track your movement. He is here.

MediumReverse glass painting with precious stones embedded into the surface
MaterialsDiamonds, sapphires, emeralds, pearls, gold leaf, coloured stained glass
OccasionCommissioned for the Nizam's 50th Birthday celebrations
ScienceForced perspective + concave surface geometry creates viewer-tracking optical illusion

The portrait's most striking feature is its optical illusion. The eyes and feet appear to follow the viewer as they move — achieved through a carefully engineered concave surface geometry combined with precise shading and perspective depth. As the viewing angle changes, the eyes maintain their apparent direction, creating the uncanny sensation of being observed.

This principle, known as forced perspective, is used today in holograms, 3D art installations, and immersive museum experiences. The Nizam is depicted in full royal regalia — the sky-blue ceremonial robe, a jewelled sarpech, layered pearl necklaces — all reproduced using precious stones set into the glass surface.

"This is not just a portrait — it is a combination of art, science, and illusion that brings the Nizam to life even today."

The wardrobe is the container — an engineering marvel built to house royal garments. Inside it, the garments speak of textile craftsmanship and identity. Completing the dressing ritual, the ittardan adds fragrance — the invisible garment. All of this luxury was funded by coins that powered a vast Deccan economy across six centuries. Together, these five objects form a complete portrait of royal existence: shelter for belongings, beauty upon the body, fragrance in the air, wealth as foundation, and the sovereign gaze as the final, timeless seal.
